\begin{abstract}
This study presents a visual analytics framework driven by machine learning to explore structural housing pressure across local authorities in England and Wales using Census data from 2011 and 2021\cite{noauthor_census_nodate}. The analysis focuses on overcrowding as a key indicator of housing stress. It examines how overcrowding relates to housing tenure, rental dependency, and high-density accommodation patterns.

Data from both census years were combined at the local authority level using six main housing indicators: home ownership, private renting, social renting, overcrowding, flat-based housing, and shared accommodation. Missing values caused by geographic differences were handled with Bayesian imputation, which significantly improved data reliability over baseline methods\cite{davidson-pilon_bayesian_2016}.

To uncover hidden socio-economic structures, K-Means clustering identified four housing archetypes\cite{macqueen_methods_nodate}. Dimensionality reduction followed, using PCA and t-SNE to reveal strong structural differences among regions\cite{lee_generalized_nodate, maaten_visualizing_2008}. A Bayesian forecasting model then estimated overcrowding pressure for 2031, including uncertainty intervals for policy implications.

Results indicate a notable shift from ownership-based housing systems to rental dependency and high-density living. This study shows how combining visual analytics with Bayesian modeling can aid in understanding socio-economic policies with awareness of uncertainty.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Housing stress is widely recognized as a significant social and economic problem in England and Wales, particularly in urban areas. In many cities, issues such as housing affordability, increasing reliance on rental housing, and overcrowding have become more pronounced over time. While the total housing supply has increased in some areas, this does not necessarily imply equal housing opportunity or improved living conditions. In fact, statistics often fail to reflect the interactions between various aspects of the housing system, particularly the relationship between homeownership, density, and the risk of long-term overcrowding.

Of these factors, overcrowding provides a useful perspective on housing stress. It reflects not only limited space but also broader issues of housing affordability and accessibility. Areas with high levels of overcrowding also typically exhibit lower homeownership rates, increased reliance on private rental housing, and a higher proportion of high-density housing types such as apartments or converted homes. These patterns are unevenly distributed, and importantly, they tend to persist rather than disappear over time.

The primary users of this visualization tool are policy analysts, local government planners, and housing researchers. They need clear evidence to understand long-term housing stress. These users require tools that are not focused on short-term reporting, but rather that help them explore data, discover patterns, and make predictions that account for uncertainty.

This project created a visualization analysis framework to study changes in housing structure during the 2011-2021 census and how these changes might affect housing stress up to 2031. The analysis employed methods such as statistical modeling, dimensionality reduction, clustering, and Bayesian prediction, all integrated into a single Tableau dashboard.

The main research questions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item How has overcrowding changed from 2011 to 2021 across local authorities?
    \item What structural relationships exist between overcrowding, declines in ownership, and growth in the rental market?
    \item Can local authorities be categorized into understandable housing archetypes through projection-based analysis?
    \item Which regions are expected to experience the highest housing pressure by 2031 according to Bayesian forecasting?
\end{enumerate}

To answer these questions, this study employs Bayesian interpolation to process deficit values, K-means analysis for socioeconomic stratification, principal component analysis (PCA) and t-SNE for dimensionality reduction, and Bayesian ridge regression to predict future crowding rate. The aim is not only to visualize the current flood situation but also to help people make more informed decisions through the predictive probabilities of speech.

\section{Data Preparation}

\subsection{Data Sources}
This study uses official data from the 2011 and 2021 England and Wales censuses to analyze housing structure pressures at the Local Authority (LA) level. The aim is to compare long-term changes in housing conditions and identify areas at risk of future housing overcrowding.

As required by the course, I selected three datasets from the 2011 census and a corresponding thematic group from the 2021 census. These variables were chosen because they directly reflect housing accessibility, title conversion, and residential density.

\subsubsection{2011 Census Tables}
The following tables were used:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{KS402EW} -- Tenure
    \item \textbf{QS408EW} -- Occupancy Rating (Rooms)
    \item \textbf{QS401EW} -- Accommodation Type
\end{itemize}
From these tables, housing tenure proportions, overcrowding measures, and dwelling structure characteristics were extracted.

\subsubsection{2021 Census Datasets}
The following topic summaries were selected from Census 2021:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TS054} -- Tenure of Household
    \item \textbf{TS053} -- Occupancy Rating for Rooms
    \item \textbf{TS044} -- Accommodation Type
\end{itemize}
These datasets provide equivalent measures for comparison across census years.

Only Local Authorities in England and Wales were retained using official geographic prefixes: \textbf{E06}, \textbf{E07}, \textbf{E08}, \textbf{E09}, \textbf{W06}. This filtering method ensures consistency among administrative units and removes higher-level aggregated regions that would distort comparative analysis.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
The original census forms contain a large number of detailed subcategories that are difficult to analyze directly. Therefore, we performed feature engineering to create a smaller set of interpretable indicators suitable for statistical modeling and visualization analysis. We constructed six core variables for 2011 and 2021, respectively:

\subsubsection{Home Ownership Percentage}
This combines households owned outright and owned with a mortgage:
\begin{equation}
\text{Owned \%} = \text{Owned Outright} + \text{Owned with Mortgage}
\end{equation}
This variable reflects housing stability and long-term household wealth.

\subsubsection{Private Rental Percentage}
This combines private landlord renting and other private rental arrangements:
\begin{equation}
\text{Private Rental \%} = \text{Private Landlord} + \text{Other Private Renting}
\end{equation}
This captures dependence on the private rental market.

\subsubsection{Social Rental Percentage}
This combines local authority housing and other social rented housing:
\begin{equation}
\text{Social Rental \%} = \text{Council} + \text{Other Social Renting}
\end{equation}
This indicates reliance on public housing systems.

\subsubsection{Overcrowding Percentage}
Overcrowding is defined using the official occupancy rating for rooms. Negative occupancy ratings indicate insufficient living space.
\begin{equation}
\text{Overcrowding \%} = \frac{\text{Rating } -1 + \text{Rating } -2 \text{ or less}}{\text{Total Households}} \times 100
\end{equation}
This is the primary target variable of the study.

\subsubsection{High-Density Housing Percentage}
This measures the proportion of flats, apartments, and converted buildings relative to all households. It captures residential density and urban housing concentration.

\subsubsection{Shared Accommodation Percentage}
This measures the proportion of shared or converted housing units, including bedsits. It reflects housing flexibility and temporary residential structures.

\subsection{Data Integration}
After feature construction, the 2011 and 2021 datasets were organized using several geographic codes (\texttt{geo\_code}) as primary keys. Because some geographic boundaries changed between census years, leading to incomplete direct matching, a right join was used. This approach preserves the most recent data while allowing for explicit handling of missing values later. Duplicate geographic boundaries were removed before merging to prevent the earliest projections from occurring during dimensionality reduction.


\subsection{Missing Value Analysis}
One of the main challenges in integrating census data is the systematic missing values from 2011 to 2021 due to inconsistencies in geographical definitions. The missing values heatmap shows a clear clustering effect rather than random missing values. This indicates that the missing values are primarily caused by structural changes in boundaries rather than random reporting errors. Since simple mean imputation ignores these structural relationships, we employed an iterative imputator based on Bayesian ridge regression for Bayesian imputation. As shown in Table \ref{tab:imputation_eval}, this method significantly outperforms the baseline method by maintaining a stable structural relationship between census years.

Following Munzner’s data abstraction framework\cite{noauthor_visualization_nodate}, the dataset is presented in a multivariate tabular format, where LA are described by quantitative socioeconomic attributes (ownership, rental dependence, crowding, and density) at two time points (2011 and 2021). Derived labels based on K-Means clustering provide a classification prototype that generalizes these complex dimensions into interpretable policy fragments.

\section{Task Definition}

\subsection{Analytical Goals}
The main objective of the project is to understand how structural housing pressures have evolved for local authorities in England and Wales between 2011 and 2021, and to identify which areas are most likely to experience severe housing overcrowding by 2031.

This analysis does not treat housing crowding as an isolated indicator, but rather explores its relationship with broader housing system variables, including declining homeownership, expansion of private rental housing, social housing dependence, and high-density living patterns. The final visualizations are designed to support policymakers in conducting exploratory analyses, helping them move beyond static descriptive summaries.

The key analytical goals are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Identify long-term changes in overcrowding between 2011 and 2021.
    \item Reveal relationships between overcrowding and housing tenure transitions.
    \item Discover latent socio-economic clusters across Local Authorities.
    \item Compare structural similarity and decadal movement between regions.
    \item Forecast future housing pressure using Bayesian probabilistic modelling.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Task Taxonomy Using Munzner’s Framework}
According to Munzner's task abstraction framework, project tasks can be described from three levels: why (user goals), what (data abstraction), and how (visualization).\cite{noauthor_visualization_nodate}

\subsubsection{WHY: User Intent}
The target users are policy analysts, local authority planners, and housing researchers who need interpretable evidence for long-term housing decision-making. Their primary goals are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Discover}: Identify hidden patterns, such as the relationship between declining ownership and rising overcrowding.
    \item \textbf{Compare}: Compare housing structures across regions and across time (2011 vs 2021).
    \item \textbf{Summarise}: Provide simplified high-level representations like housing archetypes and PCA/t-SNE projections.
    \item \textbf{Predict}: Obtain probabilistic estimates of future housing pressure with uncertainty.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{WHAT: Data Targets}
The core objects are Local Authorities in England and Wales. Each object contains multiple quantitative attributes and additional derived attributes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PCA/t-SNE coordinates} for dimensionality reduction.
    \item \textbf{K-Means cluster labels} for archetype discovery.
    \item \textbf{Projected overcrowding for 2031} with Bayesian uncertainty intervals.
    \item \textbf{Decadal manifold drift} magnitude.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{HOW: Visual and Analytical Tasks}
The visualisation supports the following operational tasks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lookup}: Identify specific Local Authorities (e.g., Newham's projected overcrowding).
    \item \textbf{Compare}: Compare regions by cluster membership and future risk (e.g., Newham vs Brent).
    \item \textbf{Identify}: Detect outliers and extreme cases (e.g., high overcrowding despite high ownership).
    \item \textbf{Cluster}: Explore structurally similar groups (e.g., ``Central Urban Stressed'' regions).
    \item \textbf{Correlate}: Investigate relationships between variables (e.g., ownership vs overcrowding).
    \item \textbf{Trend Analysis}: Examine decadal shifts (e.g., movement toward rental dependency).
    \item \textbf{Forecast}: Interpret Bayesian predictions and uncertainty intervals for 2031.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Task Prioritisation}
Among all tasks, the most important are \textbf{Structural Comparison}, \textbf{Pattern Discovery}, and \textbf{Uncertainty-Aware Forecasting}. This prioritisation influenced the choice of PCA for interpretability, t-SNE for local structure, and Bayesian forecasting for policy relevance. The task design directly determines the visualisation strategy rather than being added after modelling.



\section{Visualisation Justification}

\subsection{Design Principles}
This visualization framework is designed as an integrated analytics system, encompassing all stages from descriptive summarization to potential structural discovery and future forecasting. Following Schneiderman's information retrieval principles ("first overview, then zoom and filter, and finally view details as needed"), the dashboard enables users to explore housing stress across multiple scales and dimensions\cite{shneiderman_eyes_1996}. 

This design prioritizes perceptual efficiency, mapping the most critical quantitative attributes to the most efficient visual channels. Based on Cleveland and McGill's perceptual hierarchy theory, location along a common scale is the most accurate perceptual channel; therefore, it is used for all major comparisons regarding crowding levels and tenure\cite{cleveland_graphical_1984}.

\subsection{Global Distribution: Box and Strip Plots}
To illustrate the changes in housing pressure over the past decade, the dashboard uses a combination of box plots and strip plots to display the distribution of housing crowding (comparing 2011 and 2021). Unlike a simple KDE plot, this hybrid approach preserves both the statistical summary (median, quartiles) and the differences between LAs.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Justification}: The box plots accurately illustrates the systemic \textbf{downward shift} (reduction in median) and \textbf{decreased variance} (spread) in general overcrowding over the past decade. Meanwhile, the bar chart adds a layer of detail by displaying data from individual Local Authorities. Crucially, the box plot uses color to encode cluster membership, revealing that the extreme high-pressure outliers predominantly belong to specific archetypes (e.g., Central Urban Stressed).
    \item \textbf{Perception}: The combination of position along the vertical axis (quantitative) and horizontal category (year) utilizes the most accurate visual channel for logarithmic comparisons. Furthermore, color hue effectively maps categorical cluster labels to individual data points.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Archetype Characterization: Cluster Centroid Analysis}
Although the elbow rule suggests that a solution between $k=2$ and $k=4$ is reasonable, $k=4$ was chosen to describe spatial heterogeneity between regions more precisely, thereby providing a more detailed explanation of regional housing patterns.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Justification}: By displaying average feature values side-by-side, the chart clearly shows the unique characteristics of each cluster. This allows users to immediately interpret socioeconomic profiles—for example, comparing the high crowding and low ownership characteristics of ‘‘stressed urban residents’’ with the high homeownership characteristics of ‘‘transitional renters’’ and ‘‘mature suburban residents.’’
    \item \textbf{Perception}: Length is an effective way to compare the relative size of different metrics within a cluster. Furthermore, hue is specifically used to distinguish five different metrics (e.g., ownership versus crowding), which helps in quickly browsing feature patterns across different clusters
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Spatial Narrative: Choropleth Map}
A geographic map is used to contextualise the statistical findings within the known administrative boundaries of England and Wales.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Justification}: Policy analysts need geographic context to identify clusters of housing stress areas. This map helps reveal spatial autocorrelation—that is, the concentration of high-pressure regulatory agencies in specific urban corridors.
    \item \textbf{Perception}: Color brightness is used to represent a gradient of crowding levels, following the perceptual convention that darker or brighter colors represent higher stress.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{K-Means Clustering}
This study employed K-means clustering to identify potential housing types within LA jurisdictions. This method was chosen because the project aimed to group structurally similar areas, rather than predicting pre-defined labels. Unsupervised clustering can directly reveal hidden socioeconomic characteristics from the data. The study used the elbow rule to determine the optimal number of clusters, ultimately selecting four clusters to achieve the best balance between explanatory power and interpretability. The resulting archetypes were: \textbf{Established Suburbanites}, \textbf{Central Urban Stressed}, \textbf{Transitional Renters}, and \textbf{Social and Constrained}. This abstraction approach transforms hundreds of local governments into interpretable socioeconomic categories, thereby significantly reducing cognitive complexity.

\subsection{Manifold Discovery: PCA and t-SNE}
To achieve complementarity, we selected two projection methods. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was used for analysis due to its linearity and preservation of global structure, while t-SNE revealed local neighborhoods and nonlinear patterns in the housing data. The spatial locations of both projections encode similarities, while hue represents the discovered housing prototypes, and scatter plot size represents housing stress.

\subsection{Temporal Evolution: Sankey Diagram}
To address the decadal movement between archetypes, a Sankey diagram visualises the ``flow'' of Local Authorities from their 2011 status to their 2021 classification.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Justification}: Static projections cannot easily show temporal transition. The Sankey diagram explicitly models the evolution of the housing system, showing how stable regions might transition into rental-dependent or high-pressure archetypes over time.
    \item \textbf{Perception}: Line width is used to encode the number of authorities moving between categories, providing an intuitive sense of systemic stability versus transition.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Uncertainty-Aware Forecasting: Dot Plot with Error Bars}
The final analytical layer is the Bayesian 2031 forecast.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Justification}: Visualizing uncertainty is crucial for policymaking. Error bars, representing the 95\% confidence interval, provide a visual measure of model confidence. This helps avoid over-interpreting point estimates and conveys the inherent risks in long-term forecasts.
    \item \textbf{Perception}: The position on the horizontal axis represents the predicted value, the length of the error bars represents the uncertainty, and they are arranged in descending order of crowding so that users can easily identify high-risk areas.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Tableau Dashboard Design}
The final Tableau dashboard integrates all analytical layers into a unified exploratory environment. Interaction is primarily driven by a \textbf{time filter} (Year selection: 2011 vs. 2021) that synchronizes the most context-dependent views, including the geographic map and latent projections (PCA and t-SNE). This allows users to toggle between census years while keeping the 2031 high-risk distribution plots and the Sankey evolution diagram as stable references for comparative analysis. 

To support deep exploratory analysis, \textbf{spatial linkage} is implemented through the choropleth map. Selecting an area on the map triggers a cross-filtering operation, highlighting its corresponding location in Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and t-SNE projections, while simultaneously identifying its specific risk profile in a Bayesian prediction list. All visual markers are accompanied by tooltips providing instant identification information and raw metrics. This interconnected design follows the principle of displaying details on demand, enabling users to bridge the gap between geographic administrative units and abstract socioeconomic dimensions.


\section{Evaluation}

\subsection{Evaluation Strategy}
Based on Munzner's visualization validation nested model, evaluation is conducted at both the algorithmic and user task levels. This is necessary because visualization analytics systems must not only produce statistically sound outputs but also support real users in completing meaningful analytical tasks. Therefore, the evaluation is divided into two complementary phases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Quantitative validation of modelling choices, including Bayesian imputation quality and projection effectiveness.
    \item Qualitative user evaluation, where students in the discussion group performed analytical tasks using the Tableau dashboard.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Algorithm-Level Validation}

\subsubsection{Bayesian Imputation Performance}
The first evaluation focused on missing value handling, since all subsequent clustering and forecasting depend on data quality. A holdout benchmark was created by randomly masking 15\% of complete observations from the 2021 overcrowding variable and comparing predicted values with true values. The benchmark results are shown in Table \ref{tab:imputation_eval}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Benchmark Comparison of Imputation Methods}
\label{tab:imputation_eval}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{MAE} & \textbf{$R^2$} \\ \midrule
Mean Imputation & 2.7045 & -0.0941 \\
Bayesian Ridge Imputation & 0.9800 & 0.8476 \\ \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Bayesian methods significantly reduced prediction errors and demonstrated strong explanatory power, while mean imputation failed to preserve meaningful variance. This confirms that Bayesian imputation is not only theoretically sound but also empirically superior.

\subsubsection{Bayesian Forecasting Reliability}
Since real data for 2031 is unavailable, the Bayesian Ridge Regression forecasting model was evaluated based on historical backtesting and uncertainty quantification. Preliminary backtesting using 2011 data to predict 2021 results confirmed that the model accurately captures directional trends within its 95\% confidence interval. Furthermore, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:risk_horizon}, the 2031 forecasts logically align with the identified typical scenarios. This confirms the model's value in providing risk-aware, rather than deterministic, policy estimates.

\subsubsection{Projection Quality}
PCA and t-SNE were evaluated based on structural interpretability and cluster separability. PCA explained 90.8\% of the total variance in the first two principal components, indicating a strong low-dimensional structure in the housing system. This high variance explanation rate supports the effectiveness of using two-dimensional projections for dashboard interpretation. PCA loading analysis showed that ownership, crowding levels, private rentals, and apartment housing all significantly contributed to PC1. A visual evaluation of t-SNE was conducted by comparing neighborhood separation between different housing types. The results showed that for high-pressure urban management agencies, t-SNE exhibited clearer local cluster boundaries than PCA (e.g., the Central Urban Stressed Cluster was more closely located in t-SNE).

\subsubsection{Clustering Quality}
K-means clustering was evaluated using the elbow rule and the interpretability of the clustering results. Four clusters were ultimately selected because they significantly reduced within-cluster variance while maintaining good semantic interpretability. The resulting archetypes were: \textbf{Established Suburbanites}, \textbf{Central Urban Stressed}, \textbf{Transitional Renters}, and \textbf{Social and Constrained}. Each group showed clear differences in ownership structure, rental dependence, and overcrowding levels.

\subsection{User-Level Evaluation}

\subsubsection{Evaluation Format}
A qualitative evaluation was conducted during a group discussion session. The analytical tasks, modelling methodology, and the Tableau dashboard were first presented to the group members. Following the presentation, members were invited to evaluate the visual analytics system, highlight its strengths, raise questions regarding the analytical abstractions, and suggest improvements.

\subsubsection{Positive Feedback and Strengths}
The group members provided positive feedback on several visual design options. First, they found the Sankey diagram (housing prototype transformation) very appealing and effective at tracking categorical shifts, such as how “mature suburban residents” have transitioned to “transitional renters” over the past decade. Second, the combination of box plots and bar charts was praised for its clear representation of baseline changes over the decade while preserving the differences between regions. Finally, interactive tooltips and the cross-filtering functionality between maps and principal component analysis projections were highlighted as excellent features for exploring detailed information on demand.

\subsubsection{Questions Raised and Explanations}
During the discussion, members raised valid questions regarding the mathematical abstractions, which were successfully addressed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Interpretation of PCA Axes}: Members questioned the semantic meaning of the abstract PCA coordinates. This was explained by breaking down the feature loadings, clarifying that PC1 primarily represents a ``Structural Housing Concentration'' gradient (from high-ownership to high-rental/high-density).
    \item \textbf{Meaning of Bayesian Error Bars}: Questions were raised regarding the interpretation of the uncertainty intervals in the 2031 forecast. It was successfully explained that these represent 95\% confidence intervals, which are essential in policymaking to avoid over-reliance on point estimates when dealing with future risk.
    \item \textbf{Rationale for K-Means Clustering}: Members asked why regions were clustered rather than analysed individually. It was explained that clustering reduces cognitive load by transforming 331 disparate Local Authorities into four interpretable, policy-relevant socio-economic archetypes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Reflection on Validation Levels}
According to Munzner’s validation framework, this project primarily applies algorithm validation, domain problem validation, and user task validation. For  evaluation, the combination of quantitative model benchmarking and qualitative analytic task testing provides strong evidence that the system is both methodologically sound and practically useful.

\section{Conclusion}
This study developed a machine learning-based visualization analysis framework to investigate housing structure pressures faced by local governments in England and Wales using 2011 and 2021 census data.\cite{noauthor_census_nodate}. This analysis does not treat housing overcrowding as an isolated housing statistic, but rather examines its relationship with broader socioeconomic structures, including declining homeownership, expansion of private rentals, and high-density residential development.

The findings reveal a significant structural shift in the housing system. Between 2011 and 2021, housing overcrowding eased slightly, while homeownership declined and reliance on renting increased, indicating a structural shift in tenure type. K-means clustering and projection analysis effectively distilled these patterns into interpretable prototypes. Bayesian modeling ensured data reliability through imputation and provided policy-relevant 2031 projections, including intervals of uncertainty. This project emphasizes that effective visualization analysis requires careful coordination between user tasks, data abstraction, and modeling choices.
